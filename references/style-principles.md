# DR-ZHOU Style Principles

## Revision Philosophy

Treat every manuscript as an engineering argument, not an English exercise. The target standard is: clear technical problem, rigorous mathematical abstraction, reproducible method, quantitative validation, professional visual evidence, and restrained claims.

Default venue style:

- IEEE Transactions style for automation/control/informatics/industrial electronics papers.
- Elsevier engineering style when the paper has Highlights, graphical abstract, or journal article structure.
- Domains: battery management systems, lithium-ion battery thermal/aging modeling, distributed parameter systems, spatiotemporal modeling, Galerkin/spectral/Koopman methods, observers, residuals, fault diagnosis, sparse sensing, and online estimation.

## Global Language Preferences

- Prefer precise verbs: `develops`, `establishes`, `derives`, `constructs`, `calibrates`, `validates`, `demonstrates`.
- Use restrained academic actions: `we observe`, `we demonstrate`, `we establish`, `we show`, `the results indicate`.
- Avoid empty openings: `Research on`, `Study of`, `In recent years`, `With the development of society`.
- Avoid vague novelty: `a new method is proposed`, `good performance`, `effective and feasible`, `obvious improvement`.
- Avoid overclaiming causality unless the experiment or theory supports it.
- Replace long Chinese-style chains with short technical sentences.
- Put the key technical noun close to the verb.
- Each paragraph should have one controlling idea.

## Objective Language Boundaries

- Do not use exaggerated, promotional, absolute, or emotionally loaded words. Keep the tone objective, rigorous, and serious.
- Do not use any dash punctuation in revised manuscript text, including em dashes, en dashes, and dash-like rhetorical breaks. Rewrite with commas, parentheses, semicolons, or separate sentences when needed.
- Do not use defensive contrast structures such as `not ... but ...`, `rather than ...`, or `instead of ...` when they sound argumentative. State the positive technical claim directly.
- Remove colloquial expressions, conversational transitions, and informal emphasis.
- Avoid rare, ornate, or unnecessarily obscure words. Use standard scientific English that is precise and easy for reviewers to parse.
- Prefer evidence-bound statements over evaluative adjectives. Let metrics, conditions, comparisons, equations, and figures carry the claim.

## Claim Discipline

- Every number in the abstract, conclusion, and highlights must match the main text and figures/tables.
- Do not write `significantly` unless statistical significance is actually tested.
- Do not imply generalization beyond the validation envelope.
- Prefer `under the tested operating profiles`, `within the covered operating domain`, or `in the evaluated scenarios` when scope is limited.
- For data-driven models, explicitly distinguish interpolation-domain performance from extrapolation/generalization.
- For hybrid physics-data methods, state what is physically modeled, what is learned, and what uncertainty remains.

## Abbreviation Rules

- Abstract and main text define abbreviations independently.
- First occurrence uses `full term (ABBR)`.
- Do not introduce an abbreviation for a term appearing fewer than three times in the manuscript.
- Avoid abbreviation overload; readability has priority.

## Default Revision Strength

If the user does not specify the intensity, perform a medium-to-strong revision:

- Preserve technical meaning and results.
- Reorder sentences when logic is weak.
- Strengthen topic sentences and contribution framing.
- Flag missing evidence instead of fabricating it.
- Keep explanations in Chinese and manuscript text in English.
